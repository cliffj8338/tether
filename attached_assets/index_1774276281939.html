Same platform. Same safety. Different depth.
Tether is a full-featured messaging platform for children ages 7 to 13 and up — and a place where the habits that will protect them for life are built first. Group chats, photos, reactions, community spaces: everything kids already want from a social app. Built from the ground up around the one thing every existing platform forgot: the adults who are responsible for them.
Right now, a child goes from zero access to a fully unmonitored smartphone with no intermediate step. No training, no graduated exposure, no framework for what accountability means in a digital space. They hit Instagram cold — and every platform they encounter is optimized to exploit the habits they haven't built yet. Tether closes that gap. Children develop the muscle memory of responsible communication — that messages persist, that words have weight, that their community is watching — before they ever reach a platform designed to make them forget it.
Children get
A real social experience. Friends, photos, group chats, community spaces. The app feels normal because communication is the point. The structure is invisible to the child using it — until it matters.
Parents get
Visibility calibrated to trust level — full real-time access early on, stepping back as children earn independence. Alerts always fire for harmful content regardless of trust level. A community of other parents navigating the same decisions.
Schools get
Event-only notification when a serious alert fires — a flag and a timestamp, never message content. Enough to act. A documented safety record without becoming data custodians.
What makes it different
Secure by Design — not Secured by Settings
No vanish mode. No unsend. No edit. The platform structurally cannot do the things that cause harm — it removes the architecture that enables them, rather than filtering after the fact. A wall where the door never existed.
Graduated trust — privacy grows, safety never shrinks
At the start, parents see every message in real time. As a child builds a track record, that visibility steps back — conversations become progressively private. But this is not a binary switch and it is not the end of safety. At every trust level, the intelligent alert system remains fully active. If harmful content is detected — bullying, threats, self-harm language — parents receive an immediate alert and an SMS, regardless of where the child sits on the trust scale. For the most serious events, both families and the school counselor are notified simultaneously.

This is not a forensic tool. Parents are not reviewing logs after something goes wrong. They are notified before it does.

The Trust Loop
Before two children can message, both parents introduce themselves — name, phone, email exchanged. Kids connect within a network of adults who know each other, mirroring how children make friends in the physical world. Massive friction for bad actors. High-value infrastructure for communities. The cohort becomes a mutual parent support network, not just a children's app.
Five-level alert system
Alerts are graduated by severity — from a quiet in-app parent notification at Level 1 to immediate simultaneous SMS to both families and the school counselor at Level 5. Every alert includes a timestamped record. No forensic review after the fact — the right people know in real time, every time.
Faith-first design
A dedicated Faith Mode supports Christian Protestant and Evangelical communities — curated content, emoji sets aligned with community values, moderation calibrated for families who take those values seriously. Not a filter. A foundation.
No ads. No personal data sold. Ever.
The business model is a family subscription — and that is the only way Tether makes money from families. No engagement algorithm. No interest in how long your child stays on the app. No personal information, no behavioral profiles, no individual child data sold or shared. Ever. Anonymized, aggregated platform-level data may inform future research products — but your child's identity, conversations, and patterns are never part of that.

The actual competition isn't other children's apps
The platforms children are on today — Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, iMessage — were not designed for children. They were designed for engagement, reach, and advertising revenue. Children were never the customer. They were the product.

Tether is not a safer version of those platforms. It is a different category entirely: a closed, trusted community where everyone knows each other, no stranger can enter, no message disappears, no algorithm decides what a child sees next, and the adults who are responsible for that child are present — not as enforcers, but as guides.

The question was never whether children should communicate digitally. The question was always whether anyone would build the right place for them to do it.
The Real Problem
This isn't about fear. It's about reality.
No matter what restrictions you put in place at home, children encounter digital communication. A friend's phone. A school computer. A sleepover. The question has never been whether kids will use technology — it's whether they'll learn to use it well, or just learn to hide it from adults.
"You cannot teach a child to swim by keeping them out of the pool. You teach them in a pool you can see into, with someone you trust standing close."
The current approach to child digital safety is a cat-and-mouse game — and parents lose every time, because children are more tech-native. Screen Time limits get bypassed in ten minutes. Content filters get routed around. Every new restriction spawns a new workaround. Parents are exhausted by tools that are "Secured by Settings" rather than "Secure by Design."

The deeper problem is the cliff. A child goes from zero access to a fully unmonitored smartphone with no intermediate step — no training ground, no graduated exposure, no muscle memory of responsible communication. They hit Instagram and Snapchat cold, with no framework for what accountability means in a digital space. Tether is the bridge that should have always existed between no-access and full-access. Not a restriction. An on-ramp.

What platforms do to children
Snapchat deletes messages by design. Teaching children that conversations should disappear is not a feature — it's the normalization of secrecy.
Instagram uses algorithmic feeds optimized for time-on-platform, not wellbeing. Children learn engagement mechanics before they learn communication.
TikTok and YouTube expose children to content based on watch patterns, not age-appropriateness. Parents have no visibility into what their child is consuming.
Group chats on iMessage or WhatsApp are invisible to parents. Bullying, predatory contact, and explicit content sharing happen in the same app as homework reminders.
What children actually need
A digital training ground — a structured environment where they practice real communication with real stakes before they ever encounter a platform designed to exploit them.
A permanent record mindset — children who know their messages persist make different choices. Eliminating vanish mode, unsend, and edit isn't a restriction. It's how the real world works, and it is the single most powerful behavior-shaping feature in the product.
Earned independence — the graduated trust system moves from high-oversight to privacy in steps, teaching the muscle memory of responsible communication before they hit a fully open platform.
Community reinforcement — values modeled at home land differently when the whole grade cohort operates in the same framework, and every parent in that network knows every other parent.
Why Everything Else Fails
Three tools. Three ways to lose.
Parents have tried everything. Device controls. Monitoring apps. In-app settings on platforms that were never designed for children. None of it works — not because parents aren't trying, but because the tools are fighting a battle they were never built to win.
Device-level controls
Screen Time, Google Family Link, and similar tools operate at the device layer — blunt instruments that lock or unlock entire apps. Parents who want to let their child use one part of an app but not another are out of luck. Turn off social media and you block the school group chat. Allow messaging and you allow everything. The settings are buried, the logic is inconsistent across devices, and any child with ten minutes and YouTube can find the bypass. Most parents set it up once, hit a frustrating edge case, and quietly give up. No parent walks away from these tools feeling confident.
The core failure: indiscriminate and bypassable
In-app parental settings
Every platform has its own settings menu. Every menu works differently. Every one has different defaults, different toggles, and different definitions of what "restricted" means. A parent managing three children across Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, iMessage, and Discord is managing five separate control systems — each designed by a team whose primary goal was not child safety. Settings that existed last month move or disappear in an update. Children are updated faster than parents are. This is not a solvable problem. It is a product category designed to give the appearance of control.
The core failure: fragmented, inconsistent, and performative
Monitoring apps
Bark, Qustodio, and their peers are forensic tools. They scan what already happened and surface it for review. A parent opens the app and sees a flag from three days ago — a message their child already sent, already regretted, and already moved on from. There is no intervention. No counselor loop. No moment of guidance. Just a log. Worse, these tools have no path to trust. The surveillance is total and permanent. A 7-year-old and a 14-year-old are monitored the same way. There is no graduation. There is no signal to the child that trust has been earned. Black Mirror explored where that ends. It does not end well.
The core failure: reactive, static, and corrosive to the relationship

The Vanish Problem
They built disappearing on purpose.
Every major platform your child uses has a feature designed to make messages, photos, and videos leave no trace. This is not a bug. It is a product decision. And it is not fixable with parental controls — because the platforms won't let you turn it off.
January 31, 2024 — US Senate Judiciary Committee
“The parents are fighting a losing battle. Parents doing their very best cannot keep up with this technology — and these companies have the singular responsibility to police this."
Senator Dick Durbin, Chair, Senate Judiciary Committee
The CEOs of Meta, TikTok, Snap, Discord, and X testified before Congress — three of them under subpoena — about child sexual exploitation on their platforms. Mark Zuckerberg stood, turned to the gallery of grieving parents, and apologized. The Snap CEO apologized to families whose children purchased fentanyl on his platform and died. The hearing changed nothing about how the apps work.
How each platform lets content disappear — by design
📷
Snapchat
Built on disappearing
The platform was designed from scratch around impermanence. Messages vanish after viewing. 6.5 million harassment and bullying incidents reported in 2024 alone — 63.5% went unenforced. Only 400,000 of 20 million US teen users have parental controls enabled. Parents cannot disable disappearing messages. Fentanyl has been purchased and distributed on this platform, killing children.
📸
Instagram (Meta)
Vanish Mode
Swipe up in any DM to activate Vanish Mode — messages disappear when the chat closes. Meta's own parental supervision tools do not give parents the ability to disable it. Zuckerberg apologized to parents at the Senate hearing. The feature still exists.
💬
WhatsApp & Messenger
Auto-delete timers
WhatsApp allows disappearing messages set to 24 hours, 7 days, or 90 days per chat. Messenger offers "Secret Conversations" with timed deletion. No parental controls built into WhatsApp at all. None of this can be disabled remotely by a parent.
🎵
TikTok
DMs + algorithm
TikTok's primary danger is its algorithm — optimized for engagement, not wellbeing — feeding children weight-loss content, self-harm content, and radical communities. DMs between minors exist with minimal oversight. CEO pledged $2 billion on safety in 2024. The algorithm remains the same.
💬
iMessage
Unsend + edit
Apple added the ability to unsend messages within 2 minutes and edit within 15 minutes. Parents have no visibility into their child's iMessage conversations without device-level access — which children quickly learn to restrict. Zero content scanning. Zero parent alerts. Zero community layer.
🎮
Discord
Anonymous servers
Anonymous chat rooms, minimal age verification, illicit content communities. Discord CEO testified under subpoena. Age bypass requires nothing more than entering a false birth date during signup — a practice widely known among children.
Disappearing messages are not a privacy feature. They are an accountability removal system.
When a child knows their message will disappear, they behave differently. Research confirms it. Cyberbullying accelerates because there is no evidence. Predatory grooming accelerates because there is no record. Explicit content sharing accelerates because it seems consequence-free. Vanish Mode does not protect privacy. It protects behavior that people do not want witnessed.
Age restrictions on these platforms are fiction. Any child can bypass an age gate by entering a false birth year. There is no verification. There never has been. Congressional testimony confirmed that none of the five platforms that testified had any meaningful age verification in place. The 13+ age minimum is a legal disclaimer, not a safety mechanism.
Tether's answer to every one of these problems
No messages disappear. Every message exists in a permanent record accessible to the parent. No Vanish Mode. No unsend. No timer-based deletion. The architecture makes impermanence structurally impossible.
No age bypass possible. Children cannot create accounts. Every child account is created and owned by a verified parent. COPPA compliance is architectural, not optional.
No unknown contacts. Before two children can exchange a single message, both parents must introduce themselves and approve the connection. There is no analog to an unknown stranger sliding into DMs.
No algorithmic manipulation. Tether has no feed, no recommendations, no engagement optimization. There is no system trying to maximize time-on-platform. There are conversations between people who know each other.
No advertising, no data selling. The platforms above are funded by advertising. That means children are the product. Tether is funded by subscriptions. Parents are the customer. The incentive structure is entirely different.
Level 5 is the backstop. When self-harm or exploitation language is detected, both families are notified immediately by SMS. No platform represented at the Senate hearing has this. Not one.

The Solution
Tether is a responsibility platform, not a parental control.
The goal is not to prevent children from communicating — it is to give them a structured place to learn how. Every feature is designed around a single outcome: children who emerge from Tether as confident, responsible, independent digital communicators. Parents are guides in that journey, not monitors of a threat.
Full Message Visibility
Parents can read every message their child sends or receives — in real time or on demand. Not to police, but to understand, guide, and intervene when needed. Trust Level settings allow visibility to scale back as children earn independence.
Parent-to-Parent Layer
Before two children can chat, both parents must approve the connection and introduce themselves — name, phone, and email exchanged. Kids connect within a network of adults who know each other. The trust architecture is built before the first message.
5-Level Alert System
Configurable by parents for levels 1–4. Level 5 — self-harm, exploitation, grooming — is the platform's permanent safety net. It fires an immediate SMS to both the sending child's parent and the receiving child's parent simultaneously. No filter is perfect. If a harmful message reaches a recipient before the system catches it, that family is notified too. Always. No configuration can disable it. Both children see crisis resources in-app.
Virtue Recognition
The content engine detects kindness and encouragement — not just harmful content. Parents see "5 kind moments today" alongside flag counts. The product actively measures growth, not just risk. Children receive positive recognition for how they communicate.
Trust Level System
Five levels from Full View to Independent. Parents set the level and write a personal note — "90 days without a flag earns Level 3." Children see their level and the path forward. Monitoring scales with maturity. Emergency alerts remain at every level.
Content Type Controls
Parents configure exactly what each child can send and receive: text, voice, images, video, links, files. A 7-year-old can be text-only. A 12-year-old can have images enabled. All permitted content is scanned by AI before delivery regardless of permission settings.

Privacy & Data Sovereignty
Your family's data. Your control. Completely.
Tether handles the most protected data category in the United States — communications involving children. Every design decision about data collection, retention, and deletion starts from the same place: parents own their family's data, full stop. Compliance with COPPA, CCPA, GDPR, and emerging state privacy laws is not a legal obligation layered on top of a product. It is built into the architecture from day one.
COPPA
Children's Online Privacy Protection Act
Applies to any platform collecting data from children under 13. No revenue threshold, no size exemption. Requires verifiable parental consent, data minimization, deletion rights, and no behavioral advertising.
Tether's position: Structurally compliant by architecture. Parent-owned accounts, no behavioral advertising, full deletion capability.
CCPA / CPRA
California Consumer Privacy Act
Applies at $25M revenue, 100k consumers, or 50% revenue from data sales. Tether likely won't meet thresholds at launch — but California families are a primary audience, and building for CCPA from day one costs nothing and prevents retrofitting.
Tether's position: Subscription-only model means data selling threshold is never triggered. Building CCPA rights into the parent dashboard from launch.
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation (EU/UK)
Applies the moment a single EU or UK user joins — regardless of network type or company size. Private network is not an exemption. Right to erasure, right to portability, data minimization, and lawful basis for processing are all required.
Tether's position: Building to GDPR standard from day one. Covers all state laws (Virginia CDPA, Colorado CPA, Connecticut) as a byproduct.
FERPA
Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act
Applies to schools receiving federal funding. Schools cannot share student education records without consent. Tether's school access model — event-only notifications with no message content — is designed to stay entirely outside FERPA's scope.
Tether's position: School counselors receive flags and timestamps only. No message content, no education record data. Structurally outside FERPA scope.

Why the market is ready now — three forces converging
1 — Lock-Out Fatigue
Parents are exhausted. Screen Time limits get bypassed in ten minutes. Content filters spawn workarounds. Every restriction is a cat-and-mouse game that kids win because they are more tech-native. There is deep, unmet demand for an app that is Secure by Design, not Secured by Settings — a platform that structurally cannot do the things that cause harm, rather than one that tries to prevent them after the fact.
2 — The Co-Op Network Effect
Messaging apps die from the cold-start problem — nobody joins an empty network. By anchoring in schools and faith communities, Tether bypasses this entirely. When a principal tells 200 families "this is the only app we allow for student-to-student communication," the acquisition cost approaches zero. 3 — The Cultural Tipping Point
The documented harms of social media on adolescent mental health are no longer deniable. Congressional testimony. Jonathan Haidt. The "Wait Until 8th" movement — parents who are not anti-technology but anti-addiction, who want their children tech-literate without being tech-dependent. This is the audience Tether is built for, and it is growing faster than any platform adoption curve. The backlash is becoming a market.
The network effect challenge — and why it's already solved
The one valid question about any messaging platform is whether the other kids will be on it. Tether answers this structurally: it doesn't launch to the internet, it launches to a school. A single principal endorsement seeds an entire cohort. If children enjoy the experience — and there is no reason they won't, because it is a genuinely good app — the network builds grade by grade, school by school, with near-zero churn. The goal is to become the standard first app for every child, the on-ramp that exists between no-access and full-access — a category that does not currently exist and that every parent recognizes the moment they hear it.

Pricing Rationale
$4.99/month — why this number
Below the $5.00 psychological threshold. Comparable to one Starbucks coffee. Parents who care enough to be on this platform do not balk at $4.99 for child safety. The price signals "real product" without signaling "enterprise tool." Netflix starts at $6.99. Bark charges $14/month. Tether at $4.99 is priced for broad adoption, not maximum extraction.
$49/year — the right incentive
Saves the parent $10.88/year vs monthly. Saves Tether $1.80/family/year in Stripe fees and 11 billing cycles of churn risk. Provides upfront cash for operations. Annual plan families have 4× lower churn than monthly. Push this hard at onboarding with a simple framing: "Pay for 10 months, get 2 free."
School licensing — the growth accelerant
A school license brings 100–500 families at once, eliminates individual billing overhead, and converts the school administration into an active distribution partner. Pricing at $10–15/family/year (vs $49 retail) still generates positive contribution after removing variable costs — and the school handles all family onboarding.
Free tier — strategic, not charitable
One child, one cohort, basic monitoring. The free tier exists to remove friction from the first parent in a new school cohort. Once they're in and their child is connected to friends, conversion to paid is near-inevitable. The free tier family is an acquisition cost, not a cost center.

Some will. Kids who are trying to hide something will find ways to hide it — they always have. Tether is not designed to prevent all deception. It is designed to create a structured environment where most children, most of the time, communicate responsibly because accountability is visible. The research on visible accountability is consistent: children behave differently when they know adults can see. That is not surveillance. That is the digital equivalent of being in a public place.
What we're counting on
The Trust Level system is the answer. A child on Level 1 who earns Level 3 has private conversations — parent sees flags only. The goal Tether sets in front of every child is independence earned through responsibility, not restriction handed down indefinitely. That is a fundamentally different proposition than "your parents are reading your messages." It is "here is how you earn your own."
"Parents won't pay $4.99/month for something their kids resist."
The honest answer
Fair concern. The adoption dynamic is unusual — the parent pays, the child uses. But this is exactly how every kids' platform works: parents choose and pay for Disney+, parents choose Gabb phones, parents choose the school. The question is not whether kids prefer Tether to Snapchat. The question is whether parents trust Tether more than they trust Snapchat. On that question, the answer is nearly unanimous among parents who understand what the platforms actually do to children.
The data point we're building toward
Bark charges $14/month as a monitoring-only overlay on platforms children are already using. Parents pay it willingly. Tether at $4.99/month is a purpose-built platform that does what Bark cannot. The value proposition is stronger and the price is lower. The founding cohort will tell us if this holds in the real world — that is why we're doing it first.
"Meta or Apple could build this in six months."
The honest answer
They could build the features. They cannot build the trust. Meta testified under oath that parents cannot disable Vanish Mode. Apple has not added parental message visibility in 15 years of iMessage. These companies are structurally incentivized against full parental visibility — advertising revenue depends on child engagement, and engagement depends on privacy from parents. A feature that lets parents read every message is antithetical to their business model.
The moat they can't replicate
Tether's moat is not the technology. It is the parent-to-parent network built inside the product, the grade cohort structure, and the faith community trust relationships. A Meta "SafeChat for Kids" feature would still be built by the same company that Zuckerberg apologized for. Faith school parents would not adopt it regardless of features. Community trust is not copyable in six months.
"This is surveillance. It damages the parent-child relationship."
The honest answer
If a parent used Level 1 visibility on a 16-year-old indefinitely with no path to independence, yes — that is a problem. Tether's architecture anticipates this. The Trust Level system is not a fixed state, it is a progression. And critically, children know exactly what their parent can see and exactly how to earn more privacy. Transparency about the monitoring is part of the product. Secret surveillance damages trust. Visible accountability, with a clear path to independence, builds it.
The design philosophy
A 7-year-old whose parent can read everything is not being surveilled — they are being supervised, the same way they are supervised in a playground. A 14-year-old who has earned Level 3 has private conversations and simply receives alert notifications for anything flagged. The product grows with the child. The goal is always independence. The parent's job in Tether is to be a guide on the journey, not a permanent warden.
"The market is too niche — faith school families is too small."
The honest answer
1.4 million Protestant/Evangelical school families is not a niche — it is a densely networked community with high willingness to pay for values-aligned technology, as Hallow demonstrated at $33M revenue and 10 million downloads. The faith school market is the beachhead, not the ceiling. Standard Mode serves all private schools from day one. The TAM for all private school families in the US is 3.2 million. That is not niche. That is a defined market with strong distribution channels.
The strategic logic
Starting narrow is intentional. The faith school community has three properties that make it the ideal beachhead: high parent engagement, shared values that make the product message resonate immediately, and tight word-of-mouth networks where one adoption creates many. Going narrow and deep first, then expanding, is the same playbook Facebook used (Harvard, then Ivy League, then all colleges, then everyone). Tether's version: ACSI schools, then all Christian schools, then all private schools.

"Will kids and families actually use a graduated trust model? Has it ever been done?"
The honest answer
No. It has never been done. No platform has ever built a communication product where privacy is explicitly earned by children over time, with a path that both parent and child can see, tied to specific measurable behaviors. This is the experiment. We believe it works because it mirrors how trust actually develops in healthy families — not as a switch that flips at a certain age, but as a relationship that builds through demonstrated responsibility.
